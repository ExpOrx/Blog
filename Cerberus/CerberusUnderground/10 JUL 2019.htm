This report serves as an Activity Update on the actor ANDROID, whom we have tracking behind the Android banking bot dubbed Cerberus (see: Similar Reports section).


Information from our source

On June 26, 2019, our sensitive and reliable source with direct access to the actor ANDROID obtained additional information about the future updates of the malware and the actor’s previous activity:

1. The actor ANDROID used the Jabber ID androiddev@thesecure.biz (alias androiddev ) for communication and provided Jabber IDs androidsupport@thesecure.biz and androidsupport2@thesecure.biz of the service support members.

2. According to ANDROID, the malware proved to be successful targeting victims in the U.S. and was operated in private for at least two years. However, a reliable vendor of installs recently left the team and disappeared online, which encouraged the team to go public with the malware. According to ANDROID, the bot supports web-injects produced by third-party vendors (translated from Russian):
---
<…>
[source]: As to injects, will it be possible to order them from you?

[androiddev]: Yes, you can order injects
[androiddev]: We targeted the U.S.
[androiddev]: All was well
[androiddev]: Until the person who provided installs disappeared
[androiddev]: This person was also skilled in working with U.S. bank accounts
[androiddev]: In the end, we decided to enter the market
<…>
---

3. The actor claimed the latest version of the Cerberus Android bot is incapable of taking screenshots of infected devices. However, the team allegedly planned to implement a custom virtual network computing (VNC) solution based on a legitimate application (translated from Russian):

---
<…>
[source]: Is it possible to take screenshots?

[androiddev]: No, we haven’t implemented that yet
[androiddev]: We will implement VNC
[androiddev]: But with a twist
[androiddev]: It will use legitimate applications
[androiddev]: Once we’re done with that,
[androiddev]: We may write a custom VNC solution
<…>
---

4. According to the actor, the team agreed to have the bot reviewed by a former administrator of the Russian language cybercrime forum Damagelab (aka XSS), the highly respected actor Ar3s, which supposedly would add credibility to the service and boost sales. The review should have been published soon after June 9, 2019, but there still was no feedback from the actor at of the time of this report (translated from Russian):

---
<…>

[androiddev]: We seem to have reached an agreement with Ar3s for Saturday
[androiddev]: To do a test
<…>

5. Our sensitive and reliable source gained insight into the test control panel of the Cerberus Android banking bot and shared screenshots of the interface (see: Attachment control_panel.zip).


Control panel review

Reviewing the screenshots provided by the source revealed the panel was hosted at the Tor domain reil424lawk6u65o.onion and the malware version was 1.5.0.9 at the time of this report. The panel’s main page was a simple but informative dashboard listing answers to FAQs (see: Attachments 001.png to 003.png). The left side of the navigation menu provided access to six sections titled “Bots,” “Bank Logs,” “CC Logs,” “Mail logs,” “Inject list” and “Settings.”

The “Bots” section displayed brief information about each bot, including its ID, Android system version, location, infection date and IP address. It also displayed small informative icons that indicate which applications were installed on the infected device. This section allows the operator to communicate with selected bots. The operator can push notifications, send short message service (SMS) texts, forward calls, retrieve general information about bots, such as installed applications or contact lists, delete applications from the infected device and run web-injects (see: Attachments 004.png and 005.png). The operator also can manipulate any individual bot separately via the bot’s settings menu (see: Attachments 003-1.png to 003-3.png). According to our source, only two bots were listed in the panel. We believe these bots likely are test devices infected by the actor. They had the same IP address 95.217.10.207, internet service provider (ISP) Hetzner Online GmbH, Gunzenhausen, Germany, and one of the bots was associated with the phone number 79998105711.

The “Bank Logs,” “CC Logs” and “Mail logs” sections allow the user to manage sensitive information harvested by web-injects at the infected devices. The main pages of these sections displayed brief information about each bot including its ID, information on web-injects and collected information (see: Attachments 006.png to 010.png).

The “Inject list” section is the main workspace to manage web-injects available for each client and the operator can add newly developed web-injects (see: Attachment 011.png).

The “Settings” section is the main dashboard to manage so-called global settings for bots and the panel (see: Attachment 012.png).


Activity update

Since our previous report, ANDROID demonstrated a low level of activity. The actor added only five posts promoting the service and updated the main advertisement thread of the Cerberus Android banking bot. Nevertheless, we discovered the actor uploaded a video review of the malware (see: Attachment 001.mp4) to the YouTube channel youtube.com/channel/UCghqA3AH0u2VyA_izQA_buA registered June 26, 2019, under the name Cerberus Bot. The description of the video listed the links to the actor’s advertisement thread at the XSS forum and the Twitter account twitter.com/AndroidCerberus named Cerberus.
